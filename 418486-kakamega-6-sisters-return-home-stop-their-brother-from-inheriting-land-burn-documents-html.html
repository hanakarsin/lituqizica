    All these were in a quest to prevent their elder brother, George Analo, from inheriting their eight acres of land.

    According to NTV, the sisters cooperated and sold the parcel of land just a year after their father, Anthony Analo, passed away.

    However, being considered taboo in the Luhya culture to sell land having graves, George refunded money to the person who had bought the tract and continued staying in it.

    Burning documents

    George was in the process of building his house when his sisters showed up.

    He said they burned unknown substances, reportedly linked to witchcraft, to prevent him from inheriting the land.

    He added that they also placed several things in his house that was under construction.

    Further, the hexadic burned several documents believed to be the documents they used to sell the parcel of the land in question or its title deed.

    After they were done, they eloped with their mother, Dorcus Alivitsa, after realising their brother had refunded the money.

    Weighing in on the matter, the village elders said they would hold a cleansing ceremony saying their beliefs do not allow what the sisters did.

    Related incident

    In a separate story, seven sisters from Kakamega county asked High Court judge William Musyoka to revoke their right to inherit their father's land and give it to their brothers.

    The sisters told the Kakamega High Court they had no interest in owning part of the 34-acre piece of land their late father left behind.

    During the succession case, Justice Musyoka had questioned why their names were missing from the list of beneficiaries of Mzee Elisha Inyende’s land.

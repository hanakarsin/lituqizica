    Washington Olweny: Widow Provides 16 Photos in Late Nakuru Tycoon's Succession Case

    One of the wives to the late Nakuru tycoon, Washington Olweny, has provided 16 pictures to show that she was married to the tycoon.

    Court had allowed her to provide the picture as prove that she was married Olweny who died in 2016, leaving behind KSh 200 million to be shared among the widows, Norah Atieno, Phelisia Akoth and Anne Wanjiru.

    On Monday , March 22, Justice Teresia Matheka received sixteen photographs of Atieno and her children together with the late businessman as evidence in the case.

    The judge had allowed Atieno to produce 28 photographs but she only produced 16, some of which were not clear.

    Two of the photos showed her with her children, mother-in-law, Olweny and his brothers. She claimed they were taken at their house in Milimani Estate in Nakuru.

    “In the second photograph, I’m seen with Olweny outside our house during the lunch meeting with family,” she said.

    Another photo was reportedly taken in 1999 when she and Olweny were attending a baptism ceremony as godparents for their friend’s child, while the other was taken during a family outing in Nairobi and had all her five children.

    “We could not have been recognised as godparents in a baptism celebration if we were not legally married,” she said.

    Atieno also produced photos taken during the graduation of her daughter, Linda Akinyi, at Moi University. In the photo, Akinyi can be seen feeding a cake to the late Olweny.

    She also produced photos taken during the wedding of her firstborn, Lavendah Amondi, on April 25, 2008, at All Saints’ Cathedral.

    “It is clear Olweny was holding my daughter’s hand as he walked her down the aisle. In the last photo, he is holding my hand and my daughter’s,” she testified.

    During cross-examination, Atieno denied having been married to one George Odhiambo, the father of three of her children, whom she lived with for 11 years.

    However, the informed the court that Odhiambo failed to pay her bride price as required by the Luo culture, and as such she was not married to him.

    “Once Olweny paid the dowry in 1993, we were officially married and he took over the children as his own. He had all the rights,” she said.

    Atieno, a former clerk at the Kenya Breweries, narrated how she met his late husband saying they met at a political function in 1990.

    She said her father could not have accepted bride price from Olweny if she was married to another person.

    “The culture couldn’t have allowed it.”

    Atieno said she entered into a customary marriage with Olweny on February 3, 1993.

    “I went to Olweny’s house in 1992 with three children who were minors by then. I later got two children with the deceased,” she testified.

    She also disclosed to the court that she lived together with Akoth as co-wives for over six years.

    Allan Onyango, Akoth’s son, had moved to court following his father’s death seeking to lock Atieno and Wanjiru out of his father’s estate.

    Onyango wanted the High Court to declare that Olweny was only legally married to Akoth in a monogamous Christian marriage in 1966, and kick the two out of his properties.

    Atieno’s cross-examination will continue on May 25 and 31.

    Olweny who was the director of Evans Sunrise Hospital, did not leave a Will on how his properties were to be shared after his demise .

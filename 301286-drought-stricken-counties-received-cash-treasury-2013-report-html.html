    Drought-stricken counties have received most cash from Treasury since 2013 Report

    - Turkana county remains the face of hunger despite receiving at least KSh 10 billion annually - Most of the 14 drought-stricken counties have since 2013 received the largest budget allocation from Treasury

    03 Aug 2024, 00:00

    - Turkana county remains the face of hunger despite receiving at least KSh 10 billion annually

    - Most of the 14 drought-stricken counties have since 2013 received the largest budget allocation from Treasury

    - Gatundu South MP Moses Kuria said devolution was only helping transform 47 governors into billionaires

    The essence of devolution in Kenya has once again been brought into sharp focus as drought continues to ravage parts of the country.

    At the centre of attention are the priorities of county governments in arid and semi-arid areas where over a million Kenyans are staring at the risk of starvation despite their counties receiving billions of shillings from the national government annually.

    Notably, majority of the 14 counties affected by drought have since 2013 received the largest share of the annual allocation from Treasury.

    Turkana county, which is the perennial face of hunger in Kenya, has consistently received the second-highest share after Nairobi.

    For the current financial year, it will receive just over KSh 10 billion, with Nairobi getting KSh 15.6 billion out of the total equitable share of KSh 310 billion.

    Mandera and Kilifi counties, which have also been affected by the crisis, will receive slightly over KSh 10 billion this year, the same amount as Nakuru and Kakamega counties.

    The marginalised counties receive billions of shillings more in Division of Revenue and Equalisation Funds.

    Gatundu South MP Moses Kuria who sits in the National Assembly's Budget and Appropriations Committee, did not mince words as he expressed his opinion over the success of devolution last weekend.

    “Kenyans have a tendency of supporting things without thinking through. I hate devolution. It is our collective stupidity of transforming 47 men and women into billionaires. We give them between KSh 4 and KSh 20 billion every year without controls,” he stated.

    He wondered how Turkana could be the highest recipient of devolved resources yet people in the county were dying of hunger and thirst.

    Other counties greatly affected by the crisis include Samburu, Marsabit, Garissa, Isiolo, Mandera, Baringo, Kilifi, Tana River, Makueni, West Pokot, Kajiado, Kwale and Wajir.

    Between 2013 and 2018, Turkana received over KSh 48.8 billion equitable share alone, including KSh10 billion each in 2015-2016, 2016-2017 and 2017-2018.

    Over the same period, Mandera county received over KSh 44 billion, Kilifi County over KSh39 billion while Wajir received over KSh36 billion.

    While many on social media, just like Kuria, believe devolution has sunk the country deeper into problems, others feel the idea is rather progressive and it is the county managers who are to shoulder the blame.

    “Personally, I celebrate devolution. We only need to tighten our controls and all shall be well. It may appear that devolution started the other day (2013) but this dates back to 2003 when CDF was introduced by Kibaki Govt. It was and still is indeed the best thing that ever happened to Kenyan(s). Proper management of public funds needs to be realized and all shall be well,” noted Komen SK.“Devolution works. Just be stringent on how the money is spent. Wouldn't want a situation where a whole county waits for a whole year to have a president come and launch a culvert,” added Dennis Tirop.Lawrence Opuk said the idea is okay but lamented that the county managers are “worse than terrorists”.
